History of Valdemārpils

Valdemārpils was founded near the Dupurkalns hillfort (47 m) in the territory of the ancient Livonian Ārlava castle district , which was conquered by the Curonians in the 11th–13th centuries . The prehistoric importance of this place is evidenced by the Elku linden tree in the Sasmaka manor park, the cult site on Ozolu Svētkalns and the " devil's boats " characteristic of Scandinavian culture in the vicinity of the city. Until the 13th century, the Ārlava castle district ( Arowelle ) belonged to Vanema land and was mentioned in the 1231 treaty between the Curonians and the papal legate Baldwin of Alna.

The name of Sasmaka ( Sansmagen ) was first mentioned in writing in the tax lists of 1582, and in 1644 Sasmaka gained the status of a free village. In 1656, Duke Jacob bought the lease rights to the Piltene region and granted the village of Sasmaka town rights. During the Great Plague epidemic that began during the Great Northern War , a large part of its inhabitants died in 1710–1711. In 1834, the administration of the Courland Governorate granted the village town status again, and at the end of the 19th century, next to the Lutheran church, there was also a Jewish synagogue and an Orthodox church (built in 1887–1889). In 1894, Sasmaka gained the right to self-government.

In 1917, Sasmaka was granted city rights. In 1916–1917, a narrow-gauge railway branch Valdgale-Roja was built through Valdemārpils . In 1926 , Sasmaka was renamed Valdemārpils. In the summer of 1941, during the German occupation of Latvia , Latvian self-defense units killed all 150 Jews in the city on Nazi orders .

Geography

Valdemārpils is located on the Dundaga Upland of the North Curonian Spit on the western shore of Lake Sasmaka . The city is located on a flat massif of hills, which rises to 58 m above sea level. The lake occupies a long and narrow depression of a glacial outcrop, which narrows opposite Valdemārpils so that a road passes across it. The northern part of the lake is often called Ārlava Lake, the southern part — Valdemārpils Lake. [ 6 ] Valdemārpils is surrounded by Ārlava parish . The town is crossed by the P126 Valdgale - Roja road .

Population

Around the middle of the 19th century, Sasmak had almost the same number of inhabitants as Talsi , of whom approximately 84% were Jews . This can be explained by the fact that Jews were allowed to settle in the towns of the Duchy of Courland only after 1799, when the duchy was part of the Russian Empire . In Sasmak, which belonged to the Piltene region and had the status of a free village, Jews could live there even earlier. [ 7 ] The village continued to grow until the First World War , when it had about 2,000 inhabitants, but in 1920, when it was granted city rights, only 959 inhabitants remained. [ 8 ] During this time, the number of Jews and Germans in the town especially decreased. Over the past 100 years, the number of inhabitants in Valdemarpils has changed little.

Valdemārpils Town Center

The Lutheran Church stands as a centerpiece of Valdemārpils' town center, showcasing impressive 19th-century architecture. The church's spire has been a landmark for generations and continues to be a symbol of the town's spiritual and cultural heritage.

×

Valdemārpils Historic District

The historic district, centered around Lielā iela (Main Street), features preserved 19th-century architecture and charming streetscapes. This area showcases traditional Latvian building styles and serves as a living museum of the town's architectural heritage.

Valdemārpils Local Landmark

The local landmarks of Valdemārpils include historic buildings, monuments, and natural features that tell the story of the town's development through the centuries. Each landmark contributes to the unique character of this charming Latvian town.
